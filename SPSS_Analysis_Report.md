# Household Survey Evidence on Socioeconomic Position, Care Access, and Health Limitation: An SPSS Analysis

**Analysis of household survey data (N = 173)**

---

## Background

Functional limitation and diagnosed illness often appear together, but they respond to different mixes of social and economic pressure. When daily tasks require help, household budgets and nearby services determine whether needs are met quickly or postponed. Longitudinal evidence associates lower socioeconomic position with faster buildup of physical and mental morbidity in adulthood (Kivimäki et al., 2020), and work on older populations shows income- and ethnicity-related gaps in functioning that persist beyond clinical controls (Louie & Ward, 2011).

The present file records wide variation in personal and household money flows. That variation matters because treatment bills can crowd out food, transport, or paid help. Health-service research from Yunnan links residence, earnings, and job type to multiple chronic diagnoses and their cost burden (Wang et al., 2023), while Bangladeshi studies show rural families face catastrophic medical spending that pushes households deeper into poverty (Rahman et al., 2024).

Rules about who may travel, spend, or decide on care shape whether problems are caught early. WHO summaries on gender and health emphasize unequal decision power as a barrier to prevention (WHO, 2023). Stigma and discrimination frameworks describe how financial dependence and permission norms delay treatment in resource-constrained settings (Stangl et al., 2021). Multi-country DHS modelling further reports higher odds of access difficulties for rural women, poorer wealth groups, and the uninsured (Ahinkorah et al., 2024).

Maltreatment may influence health through stress physiology and deferred care. Syntheses connect partner violence to pain, cardiometabolic illness, and reduced independence (Lagdon et al., 2014; National Center for Health Research, 2018). Rural service environments add friction—fewer facilities, longer travel, thinner staffing—which reviews link to preventable hospital use (Loria & Camacho, 2025; Govindarajan & Bhatt, 2022).

This report profiles the 173 interviews on demographics and finances, then tests whether residence, sex, abuse history, and two access items align with disease reporting and physical dependency among respondents who reached the health module.

---

## Objectives of the Study

1. Describe age, sex, residence, occupation (where answered), personal and household income, and monthly spending for all respondents.
2. Examine whether structural factors—especially residence and economic indicators—coincide with reported physical dependency in the health module subsample.
3. Assess whether abuse history and healthcare access barriers (permission to see a doctor; obtaining money for treatment) relate to disease and dependency outcomes.
4. Compare vulnerable subgroups and translate findings into practical intervention priorities.

## Association Analysis: Crosstab and Chi-Square Test

### Disease status (Q134.3)

**Sex × disease (n = 59)**  
Males: 92.6% without disease, 7.4% with disease (n = 27). Females: 90.6% without, 9.4% with (n = 32). Three of five diseased cases are female. Pearson χ²(1) = 0.000, p = 1.000; Fisher’s exact p = 1.000. Minimum expected count 2.29; half of cells below five—exact test preferred. **No significant association.**

**Residence × disease (n = 60)**  
Rural and urban percentages are virtually identical (roughly 8–9% with disease). χ²(1) = 0.006, p = 0.936; Fisher p = 1.000. **No significant association.**

**Abuse × disease (n = 60)**  
χ²(2) = 0.189, p = 0.910; most cells have expected counts under five. **No significant association.**

**Permission × disease (n = 60)**  
χ²(1) = 0.000, p = 1.000; Fisher p = 0.524. **No significant association.**

**Money for treatment × disease (n = 60)**  
χ²(1) = 0.418, p = 0.518; Fisher p = 0.346. **No significant association.**

### Physical dependency (Q134.2)

**Sex × dependency (n = 59)**  
χ²(1) = 0.414, p = 0.520; Fisher p = 0.518. Women account for eight of twelve dependent cases descriptively, but the test does not reach significance. **No significant association.**

**Residence × dependency (n = 60)**  
χ²(1) = 0.533, p = 0.465; Fisher p = 0.340. **No significant association.**

**Abuse × dependency (n = 60)**  
χ²(2) = 4.111, p = 0.128. Spearman ρ = −0.260, p = 0.044 for ordered abuse versus dependency—suggestive trend only; omnibus test remains non-significant and cells are sparse (minimum expected 0.20).

**Permission × dependency (n = 60)**  
χ²(1) = 0.009, p = 0.924; Fisher p = 1.000. **No significant association.**

**Money × dependency (n = 60)**  
χ²(1) = 0.000, p = 1.000; Fisher p = 1.000. **No significant association.**

## Discussion and recommendations

1. **Violence response:** With 94.8% reporting some abuse, integrate screening into primary contact points regardless of chi-square results on disease.
2. **Financial access:** Target the 23.7% who name treatment money as a big problem through subsidies, community funds, or fee waivers.
3. **Dependency support:** Plan rehabilitation and caregiver relief for the 20% reporting physical dependency within the health module.
4. **Survey design:** Reduce 65.3% missing on Q134 items by revising skip logic and enlarge diseased cell sizes before relying on logistic models.
5. **Rural services:** Descriptive rural majority (54.3%) supports maintaining mobile clinics or transport vouchers even though rural–urban outcome differences were not significant here.
